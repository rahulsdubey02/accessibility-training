1.2.2 Captions (Prerecorded)

Captions must be provided for all prerecorded audio content in synchronised media — unless the media is itself a media alternative for text and is clearly labelled as such. Captions must convey dialogue, speaker identity, and meaningful non-speech sounds.

Intent

This criterion requires captions for prerecorded synchronised media — video that has a corresponding audio track with speech, sound effects, or other auditory information. Unlike SC 1.2.1 (which covers audio-only and video-only), this criterion applies to video with audio.

Captions differ from subtitles. Subtitles translate dialogue for hearing users. Captions are for deaf and hard-of-hearing users — they must convey everything in the audio track that is needed to understand the content:

  • All spoken dialogue, verbatim
  • Speaker identification when multiple speakers are present
  • Meaningful sound effects: [DOOR SLAMS], [ALARM BEEPS], [CROWD CHEERING]
  • Music descriptions when they carry meaning: [TENSE ORCHESTRAL MUSIC]
  • Tone-of-voice cues when relevant: [SARCASTICALLY], [WHISPERING]

Captions may be closed (user-toggled, via a caption track) or open (burned into the video frame and always visible). Both satisfy the criterion — but closed captions are strongly preferred as they respect user choice.

Key Distinctions & Exemption

Captions ≠ Subtitles
Subtitles translate spoken language for hearing viewers. Captions provide a complete text equivalent of the audio track — including non-speech sounds — for users who cannot hear the audio. A video with subtitles only does not satisfy 1.2.2.
Auto-generated Captions Are Not Sufficient Alone
Platforms like YouTube generate captions automatically using speech recognition. These often contain errors — especially with proper nouns and accents — and rarely include speaker IDs or sound effects. Auto-captions must be reviewed and corrected before a video is considered accessible.
Media Alternative Exemption
If the synchronised media is itself an alternative for existing text on the same page (e.g., a video version of a written tutorial) and is clearly labelled as such, captions are not required. The text already provides the equivalent.

Fix (G87 + H95): Add a <track kind="captions"> element pointing to a WebVTT caption file. Use the default attribute so captions are on by default for users who have enabled them in their OS or browser preferences.

Remediation Notes

📁

WebVTT is the standard caption format for HTML5

Use kind="captions" (not "subtitles") so the browser signals the track contains non-speech sounds and is intended for deaf users. Caption tracks show in media players alongside the CC button.

⚙️

Use default respectfully

The default attribute activates the track for users whose OS accessibility settings request captions. Do not force captions on for all users — respect preference — but do add default so the OS setting is honoured.

🎬

Third-party video embeds

YouTube and Vimeo support caption uploads. After uploading, verify captions render correctly, toggle them on and off, and check they synchronise accurately. Do not rely on the platform's auto-generated captions without review.

Remediation Notes

🔈

What counts as a meaningful non-speech sound?

Include sounds that change how the viewer interprets the video: alarms, music mood, laughter, applause, doorbells, gunshots, or a phone ringing. Omit ambient noise that carries no informational value (general street traffic, air conditioning hum).

🏷️

Speaker identification conventions

Prefix each turn with "NAME:" or "ROLE:" — e.g., "DR. PATEL:", "INTERVIEWER:", "CUSTOMER:". When the speaker is unknown or off-camera, use a description like "MAN (off-screen):". Never leave a caption ambiguous about who is speaking.

⏱️

Sound cue timing

Place the sound cue cue slightly before the related dialogue so viewers have context before reading the words. An alarm that appears at the same time as the dialogue about evacuation gives no advance warning.

Remediation Notes

✅

Caption review workflow

1. Generate auto-captions. 2. Download the .vtt file. 3. Review against the audio in a text editor. 4. Correct errors, add speaker labels and sound cues. 5. Re-upload. Tools like Amara, Kapwing, and YouTube Studio all support this workflow.

🎯

Higher error rate for technical content

Auto-captioning accuracy drops significantly for technical terminology, acronyms, non-English names, and accented speech. For these content types, manual captioning or a professional captioning service is strongly recommended over auto-review.

📏

Caption line length and reading speed

Keep each caption cue to 1–2 lines of 32–42 characters maximum. Reading speed for captions is typically 160–180 wpm — ensure cue duration matches text length. Short cues that flash by too fast are a common F8 failure mode.

Example 4 of 5

Subtitle Track Used Instead of Caption Track

A developer adds a <track kind="subtitles"> for the English dialogue. The text includes only the spoken words — no sound effects, no music cues, no speaker IDs. While subtitles aid non-native speakers, they do not satisfy 1.2.2 because they are not designed for deaf users and omit critical non-speech audio.

Failure: F8 Technique: H95
✗ Poor Practice
Rendered output
Video content area
Welcome back. Let's continue from where we left off.
0:18 / 1:30

✗ Subtitle track — dialogue only, no sound cues or speaker IDs

HTML FAIL 
<video controls>
  <source src="webinar.mp4">

  <!-- Wrong: kind="subtitles" only
       covers spoken dialogue -->
  <track
    kind="subtitles"
    src="webinar-subtitles.vtt"
    srclang="en"
    label="English"
  >
</video>
🔊 A deaf viewer experiences Dialogue is readable, but a background music change signalling a tone shift and a notification chime are not captioned — meaning is lost

Problem (F8): kind="subtitles" signals to the browser and to users that the track is for hearing viewers who need the language translated — not for deaf users. The track omits sound effects, music, and speaker IDs that are mandatory in captions.

✓ Good Practice
Rendered output
[SOFT NOTIFICATION CHIME]
HOST: Welcome back. Let's continue from where we left off.
0:18 / 1:30

✓ Caption track — sound cue + speaker ID included

HTML PASS 
<video controls>
  <source src="webinar.mp4">

  <!-- H95: correct kind="captions"
       includes sounds + speaker IDs -->
  <track
    kind="captions"
    src="webinar-captions.vtt"
    srclang="en"
    label="English captions"
    default
  >
</video>
🔊 A deaf viewer reads "[SOFT NOTIFICATION CHIME] HOST: Welcome back. Let's continue from where we left off." — sound and speaker identity fully conveyed

Fix (H95): Change the track kind from "subtitles" to "captions" and update the .vtt file to include sound effect cues and speaker identification. The two track kinds are semantically distinct and cannot be used interchangeably for WCAG compliance.

Remediation Notes

🏷️

kind attribute values — what each means

captions: for deaf/HoH users, includes non-speech audio. subtitles: translates dialogue for hearing viewers. descriptions: audio descriptions for blind users. chapters: navigation landmarks. metadata: machine-readable data only.

🌐

Provide both subtitles and captions when needed

A video may need both: kind="captions" for English deaf users and kind="subtitles" srclang="fr" for French-speaking hearing users. Multiple <track> elements can coexist — the browser surfaces them as separate options.

📺

Caption styling and positioning

WebVTT supports positioning cues so captions do not obscure important on-screen content. Use the VTT ::cue CSS pseudo-element to style caption text. High-contrast white text on a semi-opaque black background is the broadly supported default.

Example 5 of 5

Open Captions — Baked into the Video Frame

Some platforms and workflows do not support external caption tracks. An alternative approach is open captions — captions permanently rendered into the video frame during export. They are always visible and require no player support, but cannot be turned off or restyled by the user.

Technique G93: Open captions are rendered directly into the video frame at export time. They work on any platform and in any player — no browser or player support required. The tradeoff is that users cannot turn them off or customise their appearance. Prefer closed captions (G87) when the platform supports them.

Remediation Notes

⚖️

Open vs. closed captions — choosing the right approach

Closed captions (user-controlled) are always preferred — they respect individual preference and can be resized, repositioned, and restyled. Open captions are a valid fallback when the distribution platform does not support caption tracks.

🎥

Produce two versions if budget allows

For important content, produce both: a version with open captions for platforms without caption support, and a version without for platforms that accept caption tracks. Store the .vtt file regardless — it is reusable across platforms.

🚫

Open captions must still be complete

The same requirements apply: all dialogue, speaker IDs, sound effects, and music cues. "Burned in" does not lower the quality bar — only the delivery mechanism changes. Incomplete open captions are still Failure F8.
