1.2.1 Audio-only and Video-only (Prerecorded)

For prerecorded audio-only and prerecorded video-only content, provide a text-based alternative that presents equivalent information — unless the media is itself a media alternative for text and is clearly labelled as such.

Intent

This criterion covers two distinct content types — each with its own requirement:

  • Audio-only (prerecorded): Content conveyed only through sound — such as a podcast, press conference recording, or narrated audio clip — must have a full text transcript that captures all spoken words, identifies speakers by name or role, and describes significant non-speech sounds (e.g., [APPLAUSE], [MUSIC]).
  • Video-only (prerecorded): Content conveyed only through visuals with no meaningful audio — such as a silent screencast, animated infographic, or muted product walkthrough — must have either a text description of the visual information, or an audio track that narrates equivalent content.

Text alternatives enable users who are deaf, hard-of-hearing, or deaf-blind to access the same information through their preferred modality. They also benefit users in sound-sensitive environments and improve content discoverability via search indexing.

Key Notes & Exemption

Media Alternative Exemption
If the audio-only or video-only content is itself an alternative for existing text on the same page (e.g., a "listen to this article" audio version alongside the full written article) and is clearly labelled as such, no additional text alternative is required.
Equivalence Is the Standard
A transcript must convey the same information as the audio — including every spoken statement, figure, and significant sound. For video-only, the text or audio alternative must cover all visual information, not just a title or summary.
Two Valid Options for Video-only
A text description (G159) or an audio narration track (G166) both satisfy this criterion for video-only content. The audio track option is especially natural for animated content where narration follows the visual flow.

Examples

Example 1 of 5

Audio-only — Podcast Episode with No Transcript

A company publishes a 30-minute accessibility podcast episode embedded with an <audio> element. No transcript is provided anywhere on the page — making the entire spoken content inaccessible to deaf and hard-of-hearing users.

Failure: F67 Technique: G158
✗ Poor Practice
Rendered output

Episode 12: Building Accessible Forms

00:00
32:47

✗ No transcript provided on this page

HTML FAIL 
<h2>Episode 12: Building Accessible Forms</h2>
<audio controls src="ep12.mp3">
  Your browser does not support audio.
</audio>

<!-- No transcript provided anywhere -->
🔊 A deaf user finds "Audio, Episode 12: Building Accessible Forms" — the episode's 30 minutes of spoken content is completely inaccessible

Problem (F67): The audio player is present but no equivalent text alternative exists anywhere. A deaf user or someone unable to play audio has no access to the episode's content. This is a complete barrier under SC 1.2.1.

✓ Good Practice
Rendered output

Episode 12: Building Accessible Forms

00:00
32:47
Read transcript

[INTRO MUSIC FADES]

HOST (Maya Chen): Welcome to A11y Talks, episode 12.

GUEST (Sam Rivera): Thanks for having me…

[transcript continues…]

HTML PASS 
<h2>Episode 12: Building Accessible Forms</h2>
<audio controls src="ep12.mp3">
  Your browser does not support audio.
</audio>

<!-- G158: full transcript immediately follows -->
<details>
  <summary>Read transcript</summary>
  <div class="transcript">
    <p><strong>[INTRO MUSIC FADES]</strong></p>
    <p>
      <strong>HOST (Maya Chen):</strong>
      Welcome to A11y Talks, episode 12.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>GUEST (Sam Rivera):</strong>
      Thanks for having me…
    </p>
  </div>
</details>
🔊 A deaf user finds "Read transcript, collapsed, disclosure triangle" — activates to reveal full verbatim content with speaker labels

Fix (G158): Provide a full verbatim transcript immediately adjacent to the audio. A <details>/<summary> disclosure keeps the page clean while ensuring the transcript is always programmatically accessible.

Remediation Notes

📝

Transcripts must capture everything

Include all spoken words verbatim, identify every speaker by name or role, and describe significant non-speech sounds in brackets: [APPLAUSE], [MUSIC], [PHONE RINGS]. Omitting any spoken content is Failure F67.

📍

Placement matters

The transcript must be immediately adjacent to the audio element, or reachable via a clearly labelled link on the same page. A transcript buried in a separate site section is not sufficient.

🤖

Automated transcription needs review

Tools like Whisper, Otter.ai, or Azure Speech produce initial transcripts quickly. Always review for accuracy — especially for proper nouns, technical terms, and speaker attributions — before publishing.

Example 2 of 5

Audio-only — Placeholder Description Instead of Transcript

A quarterly earnings call is recorded and published as audio. The page includes a paragraph saying what the recording is about — but this is a description of the audio's subject, not a transcript of its content. Under Failure F30, a text alternative that does not convey equivalent information fails this criterion.

Failure: F30 Technique: G158
✗ Poor Practice
Rendered output

Q3 2024 Earnings Call

00:00
48:12

"This recording contains our Q3 2024 earnings call with CEO Jane Smith and CFO Robert Lee, covering financial results and company outlook."

HTML FAIL 
<audio controls src="q3-earnings.mp3"></audio>

<!-- F30: a description of the audio,
     not a transcript of its content -->
<p>
  This recording contains our Q3 2024
  earnings call with CEO Jane Smith and
  CFO Robert Lee, covering financial
  results and company outlook.
</p>
🔊 A deaf user reads "This recording contains our Q3 2024 earnings call…" — the actual financial figures, decisions, and Q&A statements remain completely inaccessible

Problem (F30): The paragraph describes what the audio is about but conveys none of the actual content. Revenue figures, strategic decisions, and analyst Q&A responses — the entire informational value of the call — are inaccessible to deaf and hard-of-hearing users.

✓ Good Practice
Rendered output

Q3 2024 Earnings Call

00:00
48:12
Full transcript

OPERATOR: Good morning. Welcome to the Q3 2024 earnings call. [PAUSE]

J. SMITH (CEO): Thank you. Revenue grew 14% year-over-year to $2.3 billion…

[full transcript continues…]

HTML PASS 
<audio controls src="q3-earnings.mp3"></audio>

<!-- G158: verbatim speaker-labelled transcript -->
<section aria-label="Full transcript">
  <h3>Full transcript</h3>

  <p>
    <strong>OPERATOR:</strong>
    Good morning. Welcome to the
    Q3 2024 earnings call. [PAUSE]
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>J. SMITH (CEO):</strong>
    Thank you. Revenue grew 14%
    year-over-year to $2.3 billion…
  </p>
</section>
🔊 A deaf user reads "OPERATOR: Good morning… J. SMITH (CEO): Revenue grew 14% year-over-year to $2.3 billion…" — all financial detail fully accessible

Fix (G158): Replace the subject summary with a full verbatim, speaker-labelled transcript. Every statement, figure, and audience question must appear so the text conveys the same information as the recording itself.

Remediation Notes

⚖️

Equivalence is the test

Ask: could a user who only reads the transcript come away knowing everything a user who listened would know? If not — if any figure, name, or decision is missing — the transcript fails the equivalence test.

🏷️

Label every speaker clearly

Use "JANE SMITH (CEO):", "INTERVIEWER:", "AUDIENCE MEMBER:", or "SPEAKER 1:" at the start of every turn. This allows users to follow a multi-voice conversation without audio.

🔔

Include meaningful non-speech sounds

Note sounds that carry meaning — [APPLAUSE], [LAUGHTER], [PHONE RINGS], [LONG PAUSE] — in square brackets inline with the text. Pure ambient background noise can be omitted.

Example 3 of 5

Video-only — Silent Screen Recording with No Text Alternative

A software company publishes a silent screen recording demonstrating how to configure privacy settings. The video has no audio track, no captions, and no text description anywhere on the page. A blind user, or anyone unable to play video, cannot access the procedure being shown.

Failure: F67 Technique: G159
✗ Poor Practice
Rendered output
settings/privacy
Save changes
0:00 / 1:24

✗ Silent video — no text description provided

HTML FAIL 
<video controls src="privacy-settings.mp4">
  Your browser does not support video.
</video>

<!-- Video has no audio track and no
     text alternative is provided -->
🔊 A blind user encounters "Video" — plays in silence; the UI steps and interactions shown on screen are completely inaccessible

Problem (F67): The screen recording demonstrates a sequence of UI interactions purely through visuals. A blind user or anyone unable to view the video receives no information about the procedure being shown.

✓ Good Practice
Rendered output
Save changes
0:00 / 1:24

Step-by-step description

  1. Open the Account menu in the top-right corner
  2. Click Settings from the dropdown
  3. Select the Privacy tab
  4. Check Limit data sharing
  5. Click Save changes
HTML PASS 
<video controls src="privacy-settings.mp4">
  Your browser does not support video.
</video>

<!-- G159: text alternative describing
     all visual information in the video -->
<section aria-label="Video description">
  <h3>Step-by-step description</h3>
  <ol>
    <li>Open the Account menu (top-right)</li>
    <li>Click Settings from the dropdown</li>
    <li>Select the Privacy tab</li>
    <li>Check "Limit data sharing"</li>
    <li>Click Save changes</li>
  </ol>
</section>
🔊 A blind user reads "Step-by-step description, list of 5 items. 1. Open Account menu top-right. 2. Click Settings from dropdown…" — complete procedure accessible without video

Fix (G159): Provide a text description immediately adjacent to the video that covers all visual actions shown. For instructional screencasts, a numbered list is ideal — it mirrors the visual sequence, is accessible, and doubles as useful written documentation.

Remediation Notes

👁️

Describe actions, not appearance

Write what the user needs to do: "Click Settings → Privacy → enable Limit data sharing". Describing appearance ("a blue panel slides open") is less useful and harder to follow.

🔎

Text alternatives improve SEO

A text description of a how-to video is indexed by search engines. Accessibility and discoverability align here — the same work that satisfies 1.2.1 also makes your tutorial discoverable via search.

🔗

Linking to a separate description page

If the text description lives on a dedicated guide page, link to it directly adjacent to the video: <a href="/privacy-guide">Read the step-by-step guide</a>. The link must be immediately adjacent, not buried elsewhere.

Example 4 of 5

Video-only — Adding an Audio Description Track

A medical e-learning platform publishes a silent animation demonstrating a physiotherapy exercise routine. Instead of a text transcript, the accessibility team records a narration describing every movement and adds it as an audio description track using the HTML <track> element.

Failure: F67 Technique: G166 Technique: H96
✗ Poor Practice
Rendered output
SILENT
0:00 / 2:30

✗ Silent animation — no description track added

HTML FAIL 
<video controls>
  <source
    src="shoulder-exercise.mp4"
    type="video/mp4"
  >

  <!-- No <track> element — no
       audio description provided -->
</video>
🔊 A blind user hears Complete silence — the animation plays with no narration; the exercise movements and instructions are completely inaccessible

Problem (F67): The animation plays in silence. A blind user hears nothing at all. All visual information — the exercise movements, repetition counts, and form guidance — is completely lost.

✓ Good Practice
Rendered output
AD
0:00 / 2:30

✓ "Audio description" track available — narrates all visual content in sync with the animation

HTML PASS 
<!-- G166 + H96: audio description track -->
<video controls>
  <source
    src="shoulder-exercise.mp4"
    type="video/mp4"
  >

  <!-- H96: descriptions track (WebVTT) -->
  <track
    kind="descriptions"
    src="shoulder-exercise-ad.vtt"
    srclang="en"
    label="Audio description"
  >
</video>

<!-- shoulder-exercise-ad.vtt example:

WEBVTT

00:00:02.000 --> 00:00:06.000
Stand with feet shoulder-width apart,
arms relaxed at your sides.

00:00:07.000 --> 00:00:12.000
Slowly raise both arms out to the sides
until they are level with your shoulders.
-->
🔊 A blind user hears "Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, arms relaxed at your sides. Slowly raise both arms out to the sides…" — full narration synchronised with the animation

Fix (G166 + H96): Add a <track kind="descriptions"> element pointing to a WebVTT file. Each cue is timestamped and narrates the visual content at that moment. The description plays in sync with the video, giving blind users an equivalent experience.

Remediation Notes

📻

Audio track vs. text: choose what fits

For video-only content you can satisfy 1.2.1 with either a text alternative (G159) or an audio description track (G166). Audio narration is especially natural for animated content where a live, synchronised description enhances the experience.

📋

WebVTT format for description cues

Description tracks use the same .vtt format as captions. Each cue has a timestamp range followed by the narration text. Keep cues short enough to be spoken clearly within their time window.

⚠️

Browser support for description tracks

kind="descriptions" renders text on-screen in some browsers but does not auto-play as audio. A custom JS audio renderer or a dedicated audio description player may be needed. Provide a text alternative as a fallback.

Video-only — Animated Infographic with Inadequate Description

A marketing team creates a 45-second silent animation showing Q3 sales data across five regions — bars animate in and figures appear on screen. They add a sentence below reading: "Watch our animated Q3 sales summary." This is Failure F30: the text describes the video's subject rather than its informational content.

     all data shown in the animation -->
<section aria-label="Q3 2024 sales data">
  <h3>Q3 2024 Sales by Region</h3>
  <table>
    <thead><tr>
      <th scope="col">Region</th>
      <th scope="col">Revenue</th>
    </tr></thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr><td>North</td><td>$1.2M</td></tr>
      <tr><td>South</td><td>$2.1M (highest)</td></tr>
      <tr><td>East</td><td>$1.8M</td></tr>
      <tr><td>West</td><td>$0.9M (lowest)</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Central</td><td>$1.5M</td></tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>

🔊 A blind user reads "Q3 2024 Sales by Region, table 5 rows 2 columns. North $1.2M. South $2.1M highest. East $1.8M…" — all data fully accessible

Fix (G159): Replace the vague description with a structured data table containing every figure shown in the animation. For data-driven content a table is the ideal alternative — it is machine-readable, sortable, and conveys the same information a sighted user extracts from the chart.

Remediation Notes

📊

Match the alternative format to the content type

Use a data table for animated charts, a numbered list for procedural screencasts, and prose for animated narratives. The format should reflect the structure of the information itself.

🧮

Include all data — no omissions

If the animation shows five regional figures, the text alternative must show all five. If it calls out a trend ("South is the top region"), that context must also appear. Any omission is a failure of equivalence.

✅

Dual benefit: accessibility and usability

A data table adjacent to an animated chart benefits all users — sighted users can copy figures, sort columns, and reference numbers without replaying the animation. Accessibility improvements frequently improve usability for everyone.
